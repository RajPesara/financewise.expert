How to Start Investing

Follow these steps to begin your investment journey with confidence

1

Set Clear Financial Goals

Before investing, define what you're saving for and when you'll need the money. Short-term goals (under 3 years) require different strategies than long-term goals like retirement. Establishing specific, measurable goals helps determine your investment approach, risk tolerance, and asset allocation.

2

Build Your Emergency Fund First

Before investing in the market, establish an emergency fund covering 3-6 months of essential expenses. Keep this money in a high-yield savings account for immediate access. This financial cushion ensures you won't need to sell investments at a loss during market downturns if unexpected expenses arise.

3

Choose the Right Investment Account

Select an account type based on your goals. For retirement, prioritize tax-advantaged accounts like 401(k)s and IRAs. For non-retirement goals, consider taxable brokerage accounts. The account type determines tax treatment, contribution limits, and withdrawal rules, significantly impacting long-term returns.

4

Determine Your Asset Allocation

Decide how to divide your investments among stocks, bonds, and other asset classes based on your goals, time horizon, and risk tolerance. Typically, longer time horizons allow for higher stock allocations, while shorter goals require more conservative allocations with bonds and cash equivalents.

5

Select Your Investments

Choose specific investments that align with your asset allocation plan. For most beginning investors, low-cost index funds and ETFs provide instant diversification and professional management at minimal cost. As you gain experience, you might consider individual stocks, bonds, or specialized funds based on your strategy.

6

Automate Your Investments

Set up automatic transfers from your checking account to your investment accounts. Dollar-cost averaging through regular contributions helps reduce timing risk and builds discipline. Even small, consistent investments compound significantly over time, and automation eliminates emotional decision-making.

Common Investing Questions

How much money do I need to start investing?

You can start investing with as little as $1 with brokers offering fractional shares. Many robo-advisors have low or no minimums, and some ETFs trade for less than $50 per share. The key is starting early with whatever amount you can afford to invest regularly, as compound growth significantly benefits long-term investors.

Should I pay off debt before investing?

It depends on the type of debt and interest rate. Generally, high-interest debt (like credit cards) should be paid off before investing since market returns typically won't exceed those interest rates. However, low-interest debt (like mortgages) can often be carried while investing, especially when you have access to tax-advantaged accounts like 401(k)s with employer matching.

What's the difference between stocks, ETFs, and mutual funds?

Stocks represent ownership in individual companies. ETFs (Exchange-Traded Funds) are baskets of securities that trade like stocks throughout the day and typically track indices. Mutual funds also pool money to invest in securities but trade only once daily at the market close. ETFs generally have lower expense ratios and minimum investments than mutual funds but both offer instant diversification compared to individual stocks.

How do I choose between a Roth and Traditional IRA?

The main difference is tax treatment. Traditional IRAs offer tax-deductible contributions with taxes paid on withdrawals in retirement. Roth IRAs use after-tax contributions but provide tax-free withdrawals in retirement. Generally, choose a Roth if you expect to be in a higher tax bracket in retirement, and a Traditional IRA if you expect to be in a lower bracket. Younger investors often benefit more from Roths due to longer tax-free growth periods.

What's a good asset allocation for my age?

A traditional rule of thumb is to subtract your age from 110 to determine your stock percentage (e.g., a 30-year-old would have 80% in stocks, 20% in bonds). However, this varies based on risk tolerance, goals, and time horizon. Younger investors can generally take more risk with higher stock allocations, while those nearing retirement typically shift toward more conservative allocations with higher bond percentages.
